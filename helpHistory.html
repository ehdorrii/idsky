Historical Background

The Apollo flights to the moon in the late 1960s and early 1970s were made possible only by stretching the limits of computer technology of the day. Early in the project, guidance and navigation was recognized as perhaps the most crucial element in achieving Apollo's goal. On 9 August 1961 the MIT Instrumentation Lab received the first major contract awarded under the Apollo program, to develop the guidance and navigation subsystem of the Apollo spacecraft. One of the first computer systems to utilize the new technology of integrated circuits, the project consumed 60% of the entire industrial output of ICs in the U.S. in 1963.

AC Spark Plug (later renamed Delco Electronics) of Oak Creek, WI, was the prime contractor for the CM and LM Guidance Systems. The hardware and software of the AGC was developed by the MIT Instrumentation Lab in Cambridge, MA. Production manufacture of the AGC was performed by Raytheon Corporation in Waltham, MA. The IMU and the DSKY were manufactured by Delco Electronics in Oak Creek, WI; and Kollsman Instruments Company of Elmhurst, NY manufactured the optical subsystem. Integration was accomplished by Delco.

Over the course of development, the AGC evolved considerably. The initial version was identified as Block I, and the flight version was Block II. All crewed Apollo spacecraft used the Block II AGC and DSKY. 57 Block II AGCs, and 138 Block II DSKYs were manufactured.
